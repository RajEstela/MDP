How the Team "Fetches" Your DataFor your Week 3 checklist, just having the text print to the console is enough to
prove to the supervisor that your camera works. But for Week 5, your Pi needs to act as the central brain,
talking to the STM via serial and the Android tablet via Bluetooth.  Since print() doesn't send data to other
programs, your team has to choose one of two common ways to link your camera script with their
movement/communication scripts:

Option A: The "One Big Script" Approach (Threading)Instead of running two separate Python files, you merge your 
camera code and their Bluetooth/Serial code into one master file.How it works: You run the camera in a
"background thread." When your AI sees the Bullseye, instead of typing print("Bullseye!"), you write something 
like robot_serial.write("TURN_90").

Option B: The "Socket Network" Approach (Localhost)You keep your detect_live.py completely separate from their
communication script.How it works: You set up a local Python network connection (called Sockets) inside the
Raspberry Pi. When your AI sees the Bullseye, your script sends the string "100" over an invisible local port.
Their script constantly listens on that port, hears "100", and fires off the command to the STM board.
